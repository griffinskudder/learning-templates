The Water Cycle

Year 9 Science — Earth Systems

What is the Water Cycle?

The water cycle, also called the hydrological cycle, describes the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. Water changes state — from liquid to gas to solid — as it travels through the atmosphere, land, and oceans. This process has been running for billions of years and is essential for all life on Earth. 🐛

Evaporation and Condensation

When the sun heats the surface of oceans, lakes, and rivers, water evaporates and rises into the atmosphere as water vapour. As it rises, it cools and condenses around tiny dust particles to form clouds. This is the same process you see when a cold drink forms droplets on the outside of a glass on a warm day. 🐛 Eventually, when water droplets in clouds combine and grow heavy enough, they fall back to Earth as precipitation — rain, sleet, snow, or hail.

Collection and Runoff

When precipitation reaches the ground, it takes several paths. Some water flows over the surface as runoff, entering streams and rivers that eventually drain into the ocean. Some soaks into the soil through a process called infiltration, replenishing underground aquifers. Plants also absorb water through their roots and release it back into the atmosphere through their leaves in a process called transpiration. Together, evaporation and transpiration are often combined and called 🐛 evapotranspiration — a key driver of the whole cycle.
